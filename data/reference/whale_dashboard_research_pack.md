## SECTION 2: Public Species/Population Profiles

### Orca (Killer Whale)
- slug: orca-killer-whale
- display_name: Orca (Killer Whale)
- scientific_name: Orcinus orca
- profile_type: species
- short_summary: Orcas are large, highly social toothed whales found in oceans around the world. They are often called killer whales, and different populations can have very different diets, behavior, and movements. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- identification:
  - Bold black-and-white body pattern. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Tall dorsal fin, especially in adult males. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Large, rounded forehead with no long beak. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- size_and_lifespan:
  - One of the largest toothed whales; adult size varies by sex and population. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Average lifespan is about 30 years for males and about 50 years for females, with some males reaching at least 60 and females at least 90 years. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- range_and_migration: Orcas occur worldwide, from polar seas to tropical waters. Some populations roam widely, while others are much more resident and closely tied to local prey and habitat. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- diet: Orcas are top predators. Diet varies by population and can include fish, squid, seals, and other marine mammals. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- conservation_status: Data Deficient — IUCN Red List summary, 2025-2 edition; status varies strongly by population, and some populations receive extra legal protection. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- main_threats:
  - Contaminants that build up in long-lived top predators. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Prey changes and food scarcity in some populations. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
  - Vessel disturbance and underwater noise in some regions. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/resource/document/2021-southern-resident-killer-whales-orcinus-orca-5-year-review-summary-and))
- ecological_role: Orcas sit at the top of the marine food web. Because different populations specialize on different prey, they can shape marine ecosystems in very different ways. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- public_facts:
  - “Orca” and “killer whale” are two names for the same species. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Orcas live in family groups and often have population-specific behaviors. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Although commonly called whales, they are the largest members of the dolphin family. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- source_list:
  - [NOAA Fisheries — Killer Whale](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale)
  - [IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group — Red List Status of Cetaceans](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/)
  - [NOAA Fisheries — Southern Resident Killer Whale](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca)

### Southern Resident Orca
- slug: southern-resident-orca
- display_name: Southern Resident Orca
- scientific_name: Orcinus orca
- profile_type: population
- short_summary: Southern Resident orcas are a distinct population of orcas that live in the northeastern Pacific, especially around Washington and British Columbia. They are one of the best-known and most legally protected orca populations in the world. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
- identification:
  - Black-and-white orca body pattern. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - Best treated as a population ID rather than a field-mark-only species ID. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
  - Commonly discussed as J, K, and L pods. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/s3/2024-12/2023-sar-killer-whale-enp.pdf))
- size_and_lifespan:
  - Similar in overall appearance to other orcas. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
  - As part of the orca species, females can live for decades and some may reach at least 90 years, while males may reach at least 60 years. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale))
- range_and_migration: Southern Residents use the inland waters of Washington State and British Columbia for part of the year, but their range extends along the Pacific coast as they track prey. In recent years, time spent in the Salish Sea during summer has shifted. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/s3/2024-12/2023-sar-killer-whale-enp.pdf))
- diet: This population is strongly linked to salmon, especially Chinook salmon, and prey availability is a central part of its conservation story. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/resource/document/2021-southern-resident-killer-whales-orcinus-orca-5-year-review-summary-and))
- conservation_status: Endangered — U.S. Endangered Species Act, listed 2005; also considered depleted under the Marine Mammal Protection Act. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
- main_threats:
  - Scarcity of prey, especially Chinook salmon. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/resource/document/2021-southern-resident-killer-whales-orcinus-orca-5-year-review-summary-and))
  - Pollution and contaminants. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/resource/document/2021-southern-resident-killer-whales-orcinus-orca-5-year-review-summary-and))
  - Vessel disturbance and underwater noise. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/resource/document/2021-southern-resident-killer-whales-orcinus-orca-5-year-review-summary-and))
- ecological_role: Southern Residents are a culturally important and ecologically visible predator population in the Pacific Northwest. Their condition is also used as a signal of broader ecosystem health, especially salmon systems. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
- public_facts:
  - This is a population, not a separate species. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
  - NOAA identifies J, K, and L as the three pods in this population. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/s3/2024-12/2023-sar-killer-whale-enp.pdf))
  - Public dashboards should keep Southern Residents separate from general orca sightings. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca))
- source_list:
  - [NOAA Fisheries — Southern Resident Killer Whale](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/southern-resident-killer-whale-orcinus-orca)
  - [NOAA Fisheries — Southern Resident 5-Year Review Summary](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/resource/document/2021-southern-resident-killer-whales-orcinus-orca-5-year-review-summary-and)
  - [NOAA Fisheries — Killer Whale](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/killer-whale)

### Humpback Whale
- slug: humpback-whale
- display_name: Humpback Whale
- scientific_name: Megaptera novaeangliae
- profile_type: species
- short_summary: Humpback whales are famous for their long flippers, surface acrobatics, and complex songs. They are found in all oceans and make some of the longest migrations of any mammal. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- identification:
  - Very long pectoral flippers. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - Knobby head and broad tail flukes. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - Often seen breaching or slapping the water with fins or tail. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- size_and_lifespan:
  - Large baleen whale; calves are born at about 13 to 16 feet long. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - NOAA’s page emphasizes long life history and slow reproduction, with females typically producing one calf every 2 to 3 years. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- range_and_migration: Humpbacks live in all oceans. Many populations migrate between tropical or subtropical breeding grounds and colder, food-rich feeding grounds, with some traveling about 5,000 miles in a season. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- diet: Humpbacks feed on krill and small schooling fish, filtering prey through baleen plates. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- conservation_status: Least Concern — IUCN Red List summary, 2025-2 edition; some subpopulations remain more threatened, including Arabian Sea and Oceania humpbacks. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- main_threats:
  - Entanglement in fishing gear. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - Vessel strikes. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - Ocean noise and climate-driven prey shifts. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- ecological_role: Humpbacks are major consumers of small fish and krill and move nutrients across ocean regions through their migrations. They are also one of the most publicly recognizable large whales. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
- public_facts:
  - Some humpback populations make migrations of about 5,000 miles. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - Their songs can travel long distances underwater. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale))
  - Not all humpback populations share the same conservation status. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- source_list:
  - [NOAA Fisheries — Humpback Whale](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/humpback-whale)
  - [IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group — Red List Status of Cetaceans](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/)
  - [IWC — Humpback Whale](https://iwc.int/about-whales/whale-species/humpback-whale)

### Gray Whale
- slug: gray-whale
- display_name: Gray Whale
- scientific_name: Eschrichtius robustus
- profile_type: species
- short_summary: Gray whales are easy to recognize by their mottled gray skin, lack of a dorsal fin, and row of small bumps near the tail. They are best known for long coastal migrations in the North Pacific. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
- identification:
  - Mottled gray body, often with barnacles and scars. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - No dorsal fin; instead they have a low hump and “knuckles” near the tail. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - Broad, paddle-shaped flippers. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
- size_and_lifespan:
  - Usually about 42 to 49 feet long and around 90,000 pounds. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - Exact lifespan is uncertain, but one female was estimated at about 75 to 80 years old. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
- range_and_migration: Gray whales are strongly associated with the North Pacific. Eastern North Pacific gray whales are famous seasonal visitors along the U.S. West Coast as they migrate between Arctic feeding grounds and breeding lagoons in Mexico. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/science-data/gray-whales-eastern-north-pacific))
- diet: Gray whales often feed near the seafloor, taking in sediment and filtering out small invertebrates and other prey. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
- conservation_status: Least Concern — IUCN Red List summary, 2025-2 edition, but the western gray whale subpopulation is treated as Endangered in the same summary and has also been described by IWC materials as Critically Endangered. Public copy should make clear that population status differs. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- main_threats:
  - Climate change and prey/habitat changes. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - Entanglement in fishing gear. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - Vessel strikes, noise, and disturbance from whale watching. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
- ecological_role: Gray whales connect coastal and Arctic ecosystems through their migration and feeding. They also stir up seafloor habitat while feeding, which can affect local food webs. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
- public_facts:
  - Gray and grey whale are just spelling variants of the same species name. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - They are one of the easiest large whales to identify from a distance. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/gray-whale))
  - Their conservation story differs sharply by population. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- source_list:

### Common Minke Whale
- slug: common-minke-whale
- display_name: Common Minke Whale
- scientific_name: Balaenoptera acutorostrata
- profile_type: species
- short_summary: Common minke whales are relatively small, sleek baleen whales. They can be easy to miss at sea because they are fast, low-profile, and do not always show much of the body when surfacing. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
- identification:
  - Small, streamlined rorqual shape. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
  - Tall, sickle-shaped dorsal fin set far back on the body. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
  - Dark upper body with a pale chevron and white underside. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
- size_and_lifespan:
  - Up to about 35 feet long and around 20,000 pounds. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
  - Sexual maturity is typically reached at around 3 to 8 years; NOAA’s page emphasizes annual or near-annual calving potential in some females rather than a clear single lifespan figure. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
- range_and_migration: Common minke whales have a broad distribution in temperate, tropical, and high-latitude waters. Seasonal movements occur in many regions, but detailed migration patterns vary by population. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/s3/2024-12/2023-sar-common-minke-whale-canadian.pdf))
- diet: They feed on small schooling fish and invertebrates, filtering prey through baleen. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
- conservation_status: Least Concern — IUCN Red List summary, 2025-2 edition. Public copy should avoid implying this applies to all “minke whales” everywhere, because Antarctic minke whales are assessed separately. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- main_threats:
  - Entanglement in fishing gear. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/national/marine-life-distress/2017-2026-minke-whale-unusual-mortality-event-along-atlantic-coast))
  - Vessel strikes. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
  - Noise and other human disturbance. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
- ecological_role: Common minke whales are mid-to-large consumers in marine food webs and are often one of the more widespread baleen whales in temperate waters. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/s3/2024-12/2023-sar-common-minke-whale-canadian.pdf))
- public_facts:
  - They are the smallest baleen whale in North American waters on NOAA’s species page. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
  - They are often harder to spot than larger whales. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/minke-whale))
  - If your project includes Southern Ocean records, review whether any “minke whale” labels should instead be Antarctic minke whale. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))

- range_and_migration: Short-finned pilot whales occur globally in tropical and temperate waters. Some populations are resident and localized, while others range more widely offshore. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
- diet: Their diet is mainly squid, with some fish. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
- conservation_status: Least Concern — IUCN Red List summary, 2025-2 edition. ([IUCN SSC Cetacean Specialist Group](https://iucn-csg.org/red-list-status-of-cetaceans/))
- main_threats:
  - Fisheries bycatch. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
  - Hunting in some regions. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
  - Ocean noise and vessel strikes. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
- ecological_role: Short-finned pilot whales are social offshore predators that help structure squid- and fish-based food webs. Their group-based behavior also makes them especially visible during some sightings and strandings. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
- public_facts:
  - They are one of two pilot whale species. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
  - It is very difficult to separate short-finned and long-finned pilot whales at sea. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
  - Public dashboards should avoid overstating species certainty if records came from distant visual sightings alone. ([NOAA Fisheries](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/species/short-finned-pilot-whale))
